Rental & Housing Affordability in Sydney

This project looks at the rate of rent and mortgage repayments within Sydney using Census Data. With a special focus on rent being paid towards housing authorities, and the income associated.

The Data

The data being used is taken from the 2016 and 2011 Australian Census issued by the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS). The data is displayed in the form of a Mesh Block, defined by the ABS as the "smallest geographical area." The data has been organised in a Statistical Area 1 (SA1) as group of Mesh Blocks, they are the smallest unit to release census data with population information. The population of an SA1 ranges from 200 to 800 persons with an average of around 400 per SA1. Both the 2011 and 2016 census were conducted on 9th August.

    The data in use for this article is using the:
      2016:
    • G34 Mortgage Repayment (Monthly) by Dwelling Structure
    • G36 Rent (weekly) by Landlord Type
    • G02 Selected Medians and Averages
      2011:
    • B33 Mortgage Repayment (Monthly) by Dwelling Structure
    • B34 Rent (weekly) by Landlord Type
    • B02 Selected Medians and Averages

I was then suggested to look at the data relating to social housing. Choosing to look at Social housing and mortgages within Sydney to see wheter there was a correlation whether there was less social housing around areas with higher rent and mortgaes

The Latte Line

The latte line is a perceived boundry that separates Sydney through class, education, social and economic divides. North of the border is seen as being more wealthly advantageous. The boundry is usually represented as running from the south of Sydney at the Airport to the north-east towards Parramatta.

This map shows the proportion of mortgages over $4000. The darker the area the higher the proportion of mortgages over $4000. Laying down a line across the divide shows how the proportion of wealth is distributed across Sydney.

Mortgage Difference

The data here shows the difference in mortgages between 2011 and 2016. With the Increase in repayment ammount equating to height. The difference in coulour representing the current cost of mortgaes in 2016, darker equals a higher monthly repayment.

Rent Difference

Data shown here represents the avergae median weekly rent. The height represents the increase of rent from 2011 to 2016, higher the bar, increase of rent. Darker the block represents the higher the rent in 2016. Comparing it with the difference in household income from 2011 to 2016 shows that as with correlation to the latte line houshold income has still remained higher north of the border

Social Housing

Shown here is the difference between the ammount of social housing in Sydney between 2011 and 2016.Since 2011 there has been a decrease in the use of social housing according to the data. This could be attributed for there no longer being a need for such an abundence of social housing within Sydney. It could also be attributed to a cutback in social housing funding. One interesting corelation to make is that between 2011 and 2016 there has been a decrease in social housing around Barangaroo. This can be blamed in part on the development of the Barangaroo precinct between that timeframe.

Overlaying the data on to the train network shows that most of social housing is located close within vicinity of a train line. This would be attributed to the lack of resources such as a car that most low income households may not be able to support.

Applying income data over the top of the shows that areas with the most social housing have the lowest income in 2016 and the lowest income growth between 2011 and 2016. A majority of the the housing as seen on the map is located in the west of Sydney.

I chose to overlay the income data with the social income data to better show the impoverished financial conditions shown within the western suburbs of Sydney.

Conclusion

The latte line is a signifigant problem in the development of Sydney in the coming future. For Sydney to become a livable commutable city there needs to be significant changes to the transport infrastructure. More government involvement regulating prices of rent within the city and surrounding suburbs.
